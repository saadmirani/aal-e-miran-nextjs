## About

Among the venerable lineage of Hazrat Miran Bhik, there flourished a distinguished luminary—Hazrat Miran Syed Shah Hakim Rahman Bakhsh Qadri, son of Miran Syed Safdar Bakhsh Qadri—who was esteemed in his era as an accomplished physician of exceptional skill, a spiritual recluse of profound spiritual attainment (Sahib-e-Haal Dervish), and a saint endowed with miraculous powers (Sahib-e-Karamat). A great multitude of devoted disciples flourished within this region under his spiritual guidance, and seekers from distant lands established themselves in his service and spiritual obedience. Unfortunately, precise records of his birth and death have not been preserved through documented history; however, by carefully considering familial traditions and certain chronological indicators, a reasoned estimation may be formed.

According to family tradition, his disciples were held in considerable honor throughout the region, and their remembrance was sustained upon the lips of the people for generations. Notably, Syed Safdar Bakhsh—the father of the chronicler—recounted that he had inherited the tradition that Ziya al-Din Dada (Deputy Sahib), who was the son of Syed Fayaz al-Din, possessed the distinction of having witnessed Hakim Rahman Bakhsh with his own eyes. Similarly, Manmoor Nana (Syed Manzur al-Haq) would relate that he had personally seen some of the Hakim's devoted followers. There is a recorded tradition that when the disciples would visit their spiritual mentor, they would gesture toward the family's children and remark: "These children whom you observe are indeed from among the offspring of our Pir, Hakim Sahib." Thus, special reverence and honor were extended toward his descendants among his devoted followers. There is another tradition recounting that among the Hakim's disciples was a follower from the village of Kharbiya, who later became a Sajjadah Nishin (custodian of the spiritual seat), though further details of this remain yet to be documented.

Concerning the progeny of Hakim Rahman Bakhsh, historical information indicates that he had a son named Syed Ali Husain, also known as Chand Shah. It is reported that this son served as the Chief Administrator (Mukhtar-e-Aam) of the Raja of Hazaribagh, Kamakhya Narayan. Syed Ali Husain had two sons: Raza Rasool, the elder, and Wajaahat Rasool, the younger. The younger son, Syed Wajaahat Rasool Qadri, actively participated in the struggle for independence against British colonial rule, and his passing occurred in 1946 CE. The eldest grandson of Wajaahat Rasool, Syed Khalil Ashraf, is documented to have been born in 1907 CE. By examining this genealogical sequence, it may be reasoned that Wajaahat Rasool's birth likely occurred in the final quarter of the eighteenth century, specifically between 1870–1875 CE. Accordingly, his father, Syed Ali Husain (alias Chand Shah), is estimated to have been born somewhere between 1830–1835 CE.

By further tracing this genealogical chain backward, it becomes evident through reasonable inference that Hazrat Hakim Rahman Bakhsh was born in 1787 CE. Should one consider the average lifespan of that era, it is highly probable that he passed from this mortal world sometime between 1850–1860 CE. Thus his age would have spanned approximately sixty to seventy years, which was considered a respectable lifespan according to the standards of that age. It should further be noted that in ancient times, the intervals between the births of children were often considerable, and occasionally children were taken in early mortality, thereby extending the temporal distance between successive generations. These factors must be carefully considered when examining the genealogical records.

Hakim Rahman Bakhsh possessed not only exceptional mastery in the medical science (Unani medicine), but also fulfilled the sacred responsibility of spiritual guidance and instruction within the Khanqah. Seekers from distant regions would present themselves for spiritual initiation (Bai'at), and upon receiving spiritual benefit from his presence, they would return to their native lands to serve the cause of faith. Since he was professionally a physician, respected families from neighboring regions would seek his medical expertise and healing knowledge.

According to family tradition, the Queen of Tikari Raj herself would summon him for medical consultation and treatment. Among the rulers of Tikari state during that period, the names of Raja Sundar Singh, Gopal Saran Singh, and other notable figures appear in historical records, those who were connected with this kingdom during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. However, upon careful consideration of chronological evidence, it seems most reasonable to conclude that Hakim Rahman Bakhsh likely belonged to the era of Maharaja Mitra Jit Singh, whose reign extended from the late seventeenth to the early eighteenth centuries, and who passed away in 1841 CE. Since the period of Hakim Rahman Bakhsh's medical prominence and renown also falls within this same epoch, it is highly probable that the Queen of Tikari Raj mentioned in family accounts was indeed the Maharani of that very era.

There is a recorded account that upon one occasion, the Queen was so profoundly impressed by the Hakim's exceptional skill that she expressed a desire to make him the permanent court physician with an additional honor: she offered to grant him the neighboring village of "Khatbar" as a hereditary estate (Jagir). However, Hakim Rahman Bakhsh Qadri respectfully declined this generous offer and chose instead to preserve his spiritual detachment and personal freedom over worldly rank and material possessions. Through this noble decision, he demonstrated his commitment to spiritual pursuits rather than temporal authority.

Taking into account all these traditions and chronological indicators, it may be concluded with reasonable confidence that Hazrat Miran Syed Shah Hakim Rahman Bakhsh Qadri lived between approximately 1790–1860 CE, with the tradition recording his death to have occurred on the 28th of Dhul-Hijjah. Based on circumstantial evidence, the most probable conclusion is that he departed from this transient world during this very period. His sacred tomb (Mazar) stands to this day at Miran Bighah, the seat of Hazrat Miran Bhik, bearing testimony to his spiritual eminence, his profound saintliness, and the enduring legacy of this venerable lineage.
